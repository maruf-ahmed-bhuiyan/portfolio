01 - About

A clinician-scientist with a laboratory core.

Training that moves between the ward, the bench and the surveillance system - and work that aims to keep those three in conversation with one another.

Bench · Genome · Policy

My training bridges the clinic and the bench. I earned my MBBS from Sylhet MAG Osmani Medical College, followed by a Doctor of Medicine (MD) in Virology from Bangabandhu Sheikh Mujib Medical University and a MS in Biotechnology (passed with distinction) from North South University.

I currently serve as a Laboratory Research Officer at IEDCR, characterizing the epidemiological diversity of Nipah virus strains in a BSL-2+ setting using PCR, ELISA, serology, and sequencing. Previously I led a medical college microbiology department and supervised diagnostic testing for hepatitis, HIV, HBV, HCV, CMV, and HPV during my residency.

I am now pursuing a Master of Public Health, extending my laboratory and genomic work into epidemiology, biostatistics, and evidence-based communication. My aim for doctoral research is to sit squarely at that hybrid - pathogen genomics and genomic epidemiology of pandemic-potential pathogens, combining wet-lab capability with computational biology and data science within a One Health framework.

02 - Doctoral Direction

Where I want to take this

Three directions I am seeking to pursue at doctoral level, each building directly on work already underway at IEDCR and in my published research.

01

Genomic epidemiology of pandemic-potential pathogens

Linking whole-genome sequence data to the ecological and behavioural drivers of spillover - resolving whether recurrent outbreaks such as Bangladesh's Nipah seasons represent repeated independent introductions or sustained lineage circulation.

Builds on: current IEDCR post characterising Nipah strain diversity.

02

One Health surveillance integration

Building genomic surveillance that reads across the human-animal-environment interface rather than treating them as separate streams, so that reservoir and clinical sequence data inform a single early-warning signal for pandemic-potential pathogens.

Builds on: BSL-2+ laboratory practice and national guideline authorship.

03

Climate-sensitive transmission modelling

Extending the association between climate variability and enteric hepatitis transmission into predictive models that couple meteorological data with pathogen genomic and case surveillance - targeting climate-resilient public health planning in South Asia.

Builds on: BSMMU Journal (2025) climate-hepatitis A/E study.

Featured work

Nipah virus epidemiology in Bangladesh

IEDCR BSL-2+ Genomic surveillance

At IEDCR I help characterize the epidemiological diversity of circulating Nipah strains. The virus causes seasonal, high-fatality encephalitis outbreaks concentrated in the country's central and north-western "Nipah belt," with recurrent spillover linked to the winter date-palm sap season. Transmission is driven by consumption of raw date-palm sap contaminated by fruit-bat (Pteropus) excreta, followed by person-to-person spread through close contact and healthcare exposure. Prevention therefore centres on avoiding raw sap, heating or boiling sap before consumption, protecting collection vessels from bat access, and rigorous infection-prevention and contact precautions in care settings.
